Classroom Discussion Application

This project explored how the ubiquity of mobile technology might be leveraged to (1) support the processes of teaching and learning, and (2) allow users to acquire knowledge and skills in ways currently unmet.

Project Overview

Course Title: Introduction to Human-Computer Interaction Design
Project Type: Team
Duration: 8 weeks
Methods: observations, storyboards, prototypes, heuristic evaluation, usability testing, A/B testing, statistical analysis

Ideation

Classroom observations and interviews with students revealed several unaddressed needs.

  • Asking questions in class without feeling self-conscious (students)

  • Noticing when students have questions (instructors)

  • Ensuring pertinent questions are given higher priority than the rest (students and instructors)

To address these needs, we created an academic question and answer community for mobile devices. This application allows users to submit questions anonymously, increase the popularity of questions that they consider important, and easily differentiate among questions that have been addressed from those that remain unanswered.

Storyboards

Creating storyboards required us to explicitly communicate the usage scenario, our intended user population, users’ motivations for engaging with the interface, and what users must be able to accomplish with our application. To the right are sample panels from our storyboard for this concept.

Paper Prototypes

Paper prototypes allow designers to concretely display UI elements without investing the time required to create digital mock-ups. Thus, initially implementing two distinct ideas in the form of paper prototypes enabled us to focus primarily on the concept of our design. (The following images are of Prototype 1).

Functional Prototype

We incorporated valuable feedback obtained during heuristic evaluation of our paper prototypes into a functional implementation of our system. While early designs focused on the usability of the interface for submitting questions, later iterations also refined the comments page.

User Testing

After implementing several significant changes based on the results of in-person user studies, we subsequently conducted online A/B testing using Optimizely. Measurements reflecting the number of users who added a question, the number of duplicate questions, and the time it took users to submit a question were collected and analyzed. Ultimately, we elected to include both alternatives (A and B) as options for adding new questions to the interface of the final application.
