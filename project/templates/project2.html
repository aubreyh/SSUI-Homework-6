Intelligent Radiology Tutor

Standardizing existing methods for teaching physicians to accurately interpret medical images should lead to increased rates of abnormality detection while simultaneously decreasing observational oversights and false positives.

This project focused on the development of a cognitive tutor that could help train medical students to follow a consistent process when analyzing chest radiographs (x-rays).

Project Overview

Course Title: Cognitive Modeling & Intelligent Tutoring Systems
Project Type: Individual
Duration: 8 weeks
Methods: think-alouds, interviews, task analysis, usability testing

Background

The diagnostic interpretation of medical images is a highly specialized task typically performed by individuals who have developed expertise in radiology. During the analysis phase, diagnosticians scan the anatomical structures depicted on film while simultaneously classifying image features as either normal or aberrant. The complexity of this binary decision process may be further complicated by several factors, including (1) abnormalities which appear minute, inconspicuous, and/or ambiguous, (2) the resemblance that exists among certain disease processes, and (3) the celerity with which this categorization is normally performed. Finally, findings must be integrated upon completion of the analytical stage for the purpose of recommending future action.

Think-Aloud Study

Setup

Location: UPMC Shadyside Hospital in Pittsburgh, PA

Task: Identify all visible abnormalities in a series of chest radiographs and suggest the most likely diagnosis for each case based on the findings

Participants: 2 residents, 1 physician assistant (all non-radiology specialists)

Method:

  1. Present images sequentially

  2. Record subjects’ monologues

  3. Prompt for additional information when reports are sparse or after excessive pausing

Observed Error Types

  • Missed visible abnormalities

  • Confused one disease process with another

  • Reported non-existent abnormalities

  • Made incorrect or uncertain diagnoses

  • Failed to incorporate relevant image findings into a subsequent diagnosis

  • Incorrectly applied medical terminology

  • Incorrectly described abnormal structures

  • Failed to recognize correct orientation of the image

Results

Participants’ abnormality detection accuracy on five cases with 16 prominent findings ranged from 18-38%, while their diagnostic accuracy ranged from 60-80%.

This result suggests notable difficulty detecting abnormalities and integrating findings to form a diagnosis. Further, each subject demonstrated an overreliance on patient symptomatology.

Pilot Study

Setup

Location: UPMC Presbyterian Hospital in Pittsburgh, PA

Task: Interact with the tutor prototype and provide feedback to influence future design

Participants: 3 residents (all non-radiology specialists)

Results

Participants’ abnormality detection accuracies on three cases with 10 prominent findings appear below.

Case 1 (mild congestive heart failure):
42-74%

Case 2 (normal chest): 71-86%

Case 3 (tension pneumothorax): 76-88%

Each subject’s diagnostic accuracy was 100%.

The results suggest that case interpretation was facilitated either by (1) the tutor’s scaffolding of the task, or (2) offering participants a limited number of possible diagnoses to select from.

Redesign

In response to the feedback obtained and difficulties observed, some minor revisions were made to the tutor interface.

  • Color scheme altered to improve readability

  • Third-column label changed from “Reason” to “Justification” to more clearly communicate the requirement that users must provide an explanation for structures designated as abnormal

  • “Diagnosis” combo box set as visible upon completion of prior steps to more successfully capture users’ attention and direct it toward this critical step

  • Checkboxes replaced with radio buttons (motivated by a bug in CTAT)

Tutor

The cognitive tutor was developed using the Cognitive Tutor Authoring Tools (CTAT), which include an interface toolkit with a number of components that have a predefined look and feel. It requires users to systematically assess all regions of the chest for aberrations. If features indicative of disease are present, the tutor requests that users select explanations for the abnormalities. The tutor restricts users from selecting a diagnosis until all enumerated regions have been assigned a normal/abnormal classification.

Justification column—requires users to provide an explanation as to why the region should be considered abnormal
Hint button—exists to provide users with guidance on steps where multiple responses are possible (i.e. non-binary steps)
Done button—communicates problem completion to the system

Step completion order is constrained to facilitate the establishment of a consistent method of analysis, which is thought to be crucial to the avoidance of omissions. Additionally, displaying components as they become relevant reduces cognitive load by obscuring extraneous elements.
